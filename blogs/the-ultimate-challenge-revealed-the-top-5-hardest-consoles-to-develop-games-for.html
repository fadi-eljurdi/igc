The Ultimate Challenge Revealed: The Top 5 Hardest Consoles to Develop Games for

Top 5 Hardest Consoles to Develop Games For

You’ve been thinking about it all week, when will Friday ever come so that you could spend some time playing your favorite video game?

Friday is here… You’re feeling ecstatic to get home, set-up your console, put on your favorite game, and escape reality for a couple of hours - indulging in a stress-free world. Amidst your immersion in the amazing and real-life-like graphics, a question pops to mind, how hard really is it to develop these games so that us, end-consumers could enjoy them?

Well, it’s quite an intriguing topic. But, come to think about it for a second, writing millions of lines of code for a console or PC, for that matter, is no walk in the park. The sheer amount of code needed for modern games can be astounding. Furthermore the difficulty is not only the number of lines of codes, but different consoles frequently require different optimizations and approaches due to the wide range of hardware capabilities. It's quite a difficult process that requires meticulous working knowledge of a variety of technologies. For this specific problem, lots of developers have refrained from developing games to certain consoles, which eventually lead to failure of some of them. Throughout today’s topic, we will explore the hardest consoles to develop games to.

In no specific order, here are top 10 consoles that are difficult to develop games for:

Sega Saturn

Released back in 1994, The Sega Saturn was the first Sega console to display 3d graphics.
Sega Saturn’s complicated hardware architecture however, posed a challenge to game developers. Due to its dual CPUs and multiple processors, it was difficult to optimize game performance. In order to fully utilize the capabilities of this architecture, which set it apart from other consoles of the era, developers had to acquire specialized knowledge. Developing games was made even more difficult by the fact that Sega's original Saturn development tools were not as advanced or intuitive as those for other systems.

Nintendo 64

Nintendo 64's use of cartridges rather than CDs presented one of the biggest obstacles for its developers. Although cartridges were faster to load, they had less storage space than CDs, which made it difficult to fit large game worlds and a lot of content on them. Moreover, unlike other platforms of the era, the Nintendo 64's distinct hardware architecture—particularly its reliance on specialized silicon cartridges for certain functions like audio and graphics—required developers to adopt a different approach to game development.

Playstation 3

Despite its strength, the PlayStation 3's Cell processor was intricate and distinct from the more traditional architectures of other systems. Developers had to climb a steep learning curve to realize its full potential. The process of optimizing games to fully leverage the capabilities of the Cell processor required a thorough understanding of parallel processing and specialized coding techniques. Because of this complexity as well as the PS3's distinct architecture, game development was extremely difficult.

Sony’s former CEO, Kaz Hirai even once commented: "We don't provide the 'easy to program for' console that (developers) want, because 'easy to program for' means that anybody will be able to take advantage of pretty much what the hardware can do, so then the question is, what do you do for the rest of the nine-and-a-half years?"

Dreamcast

While being ahead of its time in some aspects, the Sega Dreamcast's sudden discontinuation impacted developer support and made optimizing games for the system challenging. Its hardware was innovative but also required a deeper understanding to fully exploit its capabilities. In addition, the Dreamcast's proprietary GD-ROM format for games and its unique architecture introduced complexities for developers trying to create titles that truly utilized its potential to the fullest.
3DO Interactive Multiplayer

Also known as 3DO, the 3DO Interactive Multiplayer faced challenges in game development primarily due to its complex architecture and the absence of a standardized development environment.

Developers had to navigate different hardware configurations among 3DO manufacturers since 3DO did not release a standard hardware configuration, but
it allowed manufacturers to customize other aspects of the hardware. This included the case design, audio chip, controller ports, and even some internal components. Thus, different manufacturers produced several models with varying configurations. As a result, various manufacturers created a number of models with different specifications. developing games for the system was more difficult due to the absence of a unified platform and development tools.
